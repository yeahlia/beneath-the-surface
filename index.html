BENEATH

THE

SURFACE

The Great Pacific Garbage Patch spans three times the area of France. This massive accumulation of plastic blocks sunlight from reaching plankton, disrupts the foundation of marine ecosystems, and deceives marine animals who mistake our waste for food.

Sea turtles mistake floating plastic bags for jellyfish, their natural prey. Even small amounts of ingested plastic can cause fatal intestinal blockages. Over half of all sea turtles have consumed plastic, with younger turtles facing even higher contamination rates.

Marine crabs have adapted to our plastic waste by using bottle caps and jar lids as substitute shells. While this demonstrates remarkable adaptability, it exposes them to sharp edges and toxic chemicals. This desperate survival strategy reveals the extent of plastic contamination in marine environments.

Plastic bags drifting through ocean currents closely resemble jellyfish, leading dolphins, whales, and other large marine animals to consume them. These bags cause fatal intestinal blockages, preventing proper digestion and leading to starvation.

Abandoned fishing gear, known as "ghost nets," continues to trap and kill marine animals long after being discarded. These invisible death traps drift through ocean currents for years, entangling sharks, rays, and turtles. Ghost gear represents approximately 10% of all ocean plastic pollution.

As plastic bottles fragment, they become microplastics (particles smaller than 5mm) that enter the marine food chain. Fish and shellfish consume these particles, which then accumulate in our own food supply. Research has detected microplastics in sea salt, tap water, and human bloodstreams, creating a cycle where we consume our own waste.

Coral reefs, vital marine ecosystems, face severe threats from plastic pollution. Fishing lines and nets entangle and smother coral structures, while plastic debris increases disease susceptibility by nearly 90%. Once damaged, coral recovery requires decades, if recovery is possible at all.

On the seabed, plastics accumulate into hidden dumps.

A single bottle can take up to 450 years to fully break down,

slowly releasing chemicals the entire time.

By 2050, scientists warn that by weight,

there may be more plastic than fish in the ocean if nothing changes.

PREVENTION

Waste Management Efforts

Improving collection, disposal and treatment systems can help stop plastic leaking into the ocean.

Education and Campaigns

Raising public awareness through targeted campaigns that promote sustainable behavior and community participation will help reduce marine litter.

Technology

Developing and applying advanced tools like ocean-cleaning devices and biodegradable materials will help prevent plastics from entering and persisting in marine environments.

Incentives

Implementing incentives that motivate individuals or companies can reduce plastic production and consumption, or at least lessen it.

Recycle

Strengthening recycling systems help ensure plastic waste is effectively reprocessed.

Product Choice

Intentionally choosing products that contain microbeads (which contaminates ecosystems) and choosing safer alternatives

CLEANUP

Beach Cleanups

Volunteering to remove litter directly from coastlines before in enters the ocean, either by joining cleanups or doing it your own time.

River Intervention

Installing barriers and collection systems in rivers can help intercept waste at entry points into the sea.

Ocean Cleanup Tech

Deploying and supporting systems like floating barriers and collection machines to help capture plastics already circulating.

Retrieving Fishing Gear

Recovering lost or abandoned gear that is a high threat to marine life through entanglement and damaging the habitat.

Upcycling

Transforming collected litter into usable products help reduce waste and supports a circular economy.

Cooperation

International efforts and funding to help scale cleanup operations and protect our shared marine environment.
